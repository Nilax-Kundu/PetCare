Nutrition Guide for Pet Rabbits

March 28, 2025

A balanced diet is essential for your pet rabbit’s health, happiness, and longevity. Rabbits have sensitive digestive systems, and feeding them the wrong foods can lead to serious health issues. Here’s a complete guide to what, when, and how to feed your furry friend.

1. Hay: The Cornerstone of a Rabbit’s Diet

Unlimited access to fresh hay is the most important part of your rabbit’s diet. Hay provides essential fiber to keep their digestive tract functioning properly and helps prevent overgrown teeth.

  • Timothy hay is ideal for adult rabbits.
  • Alfalfa hay is good for young rabbits under 6 months due to its higher calcium and protein content—but not suitable for adults long-term.

2. Fresh Vegetables: Daily Variety

Leafy greens should be part of your rabbit's daily meals. Introduce new vegetables gradually and in small amounts to avoid digestive upset.

Safe greens include:
  • Romaine lettuce
  • Cilantro
  • Parsley
  • Dill
  • Bok choy
  • Kale (in moderation)

3. Pellets: Supplement, Not Staple

High-fiber pellets can be offered in small quantities. Look for plain, hay-based pellets with no added seeds, nuts, or colorful bits. Adult rabbits typically only need about 1/4 cup per 5 lbs of body weight daily.

4. Fresh Water: Always Available

Your rabbit should always have access to clean, fresh water. A heavy ceramic bowl or a water bottle can both work—just clean it daily and ensure it’s never empty.

5. Treats and Fruits: Occasional Delights

Treats should be given sparingly—no more than 1–2 tablespoons of fruit per day. Too much sugar can lead to obesity and digestive issues.

Safe fruits (in moderation):
  • Apple (no seeds)
  • Banana
  • Strawberries
  • Blueberries
  • Pineapple

6. Foods to Avoid Completely

Some foods are harmful—even fatal—for rabbits. Never feed:

  • Iceberg lettuce (contains lactucarium)
  • Chocolate
  • Avocados
  • Onions, garlic, or leeks
  • Bread, crackers, or cereal
  • Meat or dairy

7. Monitor and Adjust

Every rabbit is unique. Monitor their eating habits, weight, and droppings regularly. A healthy rabbit will have consistent appetite, energy, and round, firm droppings. Consult a rabbit-savvy vet if you notice changes.

Tip: Introduce any dietary changes slowly over 7–10 days to prevent upsetting your rabbit's stomach.
